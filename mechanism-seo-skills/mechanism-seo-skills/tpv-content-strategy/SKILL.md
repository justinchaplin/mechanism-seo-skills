# TPV Content & SEO Strategy

This skill develops content and SEO strategy for Mechanism-owned Third Party Verifier (TPV) sites. TPV content strategy is fundamentally different from PortCo content strategy — the north star is not organic conversion, it's CTR2: the rate at which TPV traffic clicks through to the PortCo.

Before using this skill, understand the two phases of a TPV and which phase you're in.

---

## What a TPV Is — and What It Isn't

A TPV is a Mechanism-owned editorial site that acts as a credible third-party voice for a portfolio company's product category. The most important word is **credible**. A TPV works because readers trust it as an independent source — not as a brand site wearing an editorial costume.

**What makes a TPV credible:**
- Real testing by real people — not copy-written product descriptions
- Honest rankings with real photos and methodology
- A voice that reads like an enthusiast or expert, not a marketer
- Willingness to acknowledge product limitations alongside strengths

**What destroys TPV credibility:**
- Language that reads promotional ("revolutionary," "best-in-class," "you'll love it")
- Rankings that are obviously engineered to push one brand
- Thin content with no real evidence of product testing
- Over-linking to the PortCo without editorial justification

Credibility is not a nice-to-have — it is the mechanism by which the TPV converts. A reader who trusts the TPV's recommendation clicks through to the PortCo. A reader who senses promotion doesn't.

---

## The Two Phases of a TPV

### Phase 1 — Verifier Page (Pre-SEO)

**This phase is not owned by the Organic team.** It is led by the CEO or Growth Lead working with the portfolio company. Mechanism Organic may help assemble or polish content, but does not drive strategy here.

**What Phase 1 is:**
A standalone landing page — typically a product comparison or ranking — designed to function as a paid ads funnel. The page is sent traffic via Google or Meta paid campaigns, and its job is to convert that traffic to the PortCo via CTR2.

**Phase 1 content requirements:**
- Real testing evidence: photos, scores, methodology
- A credible ranking — the PortCo leads, but the field is competitive enough to feel honest
- Clear, frictionless click-through to the PortCo (primary CTA)
- No SEO optimization required — this is a conversion page, not a ranking page

**What success looks like in Phase 1:**
CTR2 above benchmark. If the page is converting paid traffic to the PortCo at a meaningful rate, Phase 1 is working.

**When to move to Phase 2:**
When the PortCo is live and the brand is gaining traction — and there's a strategic case for the TPV domain to compound organically. The decision to invest in Phase 2 is a resource allocation question, not an automatic next step.

---

### Phase 2 — SEO Expansion

**This is where the Organic team owns the strategy.**

Phase 2 is the decision to invest in the TPV as a compounding organic asset — building content and links so the TPV ranks for money keywords independently, while maintaining the editorial credibility that made Phase 1 work.

**The north star:** The PortCo ranks P1 for a money keyword on its core domain. The TPV ranks P2 for the same keyword. Mechanism owns two of the top organic results simultaneously — SERPdom for the category.

**CTR2 is the primary metric throughout Phase 2.** Every content and link decision should be evaluated against: does this increase the likelihood that a visitor to the TPV clicks through to the PortCo?

---

## Phase 2 Workflow

### Step 1: Assess Whether Phase 2 Is Worth It

Before committing to a Phase 2 SEO investment, answer these questions:

**Is the core domain SEO already fully resourced?**
TPV SEO is never a substitute for core domain SEO. If the PortCo's own domain has unresourced keyword opportunities, link gaps, or underoptimized pages — fix those first. TPV SEO is expansion, not foundation.

**Is the Phase 1 page converting?**
If CTR2 on the existing verifier page is below benchmark, SEO expansion won't fix a conversion problem. The page needs to work before you build traffic to it.

**Is the SERP winnable from the TPV domain?**
Pull the TPV's current DR and referring domains from Ahrefs. Check the current top-10 results for the target money keyword. Can a domain at this authority level realistically compete? If the SERP is dominated by DR 70+ publications, the TPV may need significant link building before content investment pays off.

**Is the keyword volume worth the investment?**
TPV content takes time to rank. The category needs to be large enough that the compounding value of TPV + PortCo SERPdom justifies the resource cost over 12–24 months.

If the answer to any of these is no — document the gap and defer Phase 2 investment.

---

### Step 2: Read the PortCo Knowledge File

Extract:
- **TPV domain** — root domain, current DR, referring domains, organic traffic
- **Core money keyword** — the primary term the TPV verifier page already targets
- **CTR2 baseline** — current per-page CTR2 for the verifier page
- **PortCo core domain rankings** — where is the PortCo currently ranking for money keywords? (This defines the SERPdom gap the TPV needs to fill)

---

### Step 3: Map the Keyword Landscape for the TPV

The TPV content strategy expands outward from the core money keyword in concentric rings.

**Ring 1 — Core money keyword (already covered by the verifier page)**
The Phase 1 page. In Phase 2, this page gets optimized for organic ranking — not rewritten, but enhanced with supporting content, proper on-page structure, and links pointing to it.

**Ring 2 — Category variants and adjacent comparisons**
Keywords that expand coverage of the same category. Examples for thelaundryguru:
- "best laundry detergent sheets" → core
- "best laundry detergent pods" → Ring 2
- "best eco-friendly laundry detergent" → Ring 2
- "laundry detergent sheets vs pods" → Ring 2

These pages establish the TPV as a category authority — not just a single-product advocate — which is critical for editorial credibility and organic ranking.

**Ring 3 — Supporting informational content**
Keywords that answer questions buyers have before or during the purchase decision. These pages drive topical authority, build links, and bring in high-intent readers who then see the PortCo prominently featured. Examples:
- "are laundry detergent sheets effective?"
- "how do laundry sheets work?"
- "laundry detergent sheets pros and cons"

**Ring 4 — Linkable assets**
Content designed primarily to earn links from other sites. These aren't product roundups — they're original research, comparisons, or data-driven content that gives other sites a reason to link to the TPV domain. A stronger TPV domain means Ring 1–3 pages rank faster.

Use Ahrefs keyword research to validate volume and difficulty for each ring:

```
Ahrefs: keywords-explorer-matching-terms
keywords: [core category term]
country: us
```

Filter for: KD under 40 (for a new/young TPV domain), search volume > 500/month. Prioritize Ring 2 first, then Ring 3.

---

### Step 4: Evaluate Each Content Opportunity Against CTR2

Before adding any page to the content roadmap, ask: **does this page naturally feature the PortCo in a prominent position?**

A page about "best laundry detergent pods" — if the PortCo doesn't make pods — is a poor CTR2 investment even if it ranks well. You'll drive traffic to a page where the PortCo isn't featured.

**CTR2 evaluation filter:**
- Does this keyword have commercial intent (buyer is in or near a purchase decision)?
- Is the reader persuadable toward the PortCo — are they open to a recommendation, or locked into a specific brand?
- Can the PortCo be framed as a credible, compelling answer to what the reader is actually trying to solve?

The filter is not "does the PortCo make exactly this product." A comparison page like "laundry detergent pods vs. sheets" passes the CTR2 test for Hey Sunday — a reader comparing formats is open to being convinced, and sheets' eco-friendly, low-waste advantages are a genuinely persuasive editorial argument. A brand-specific review like "Tide Pods review" fails — the reader has already chosen a brand, the intent is narrow, and Hey Sunday appearing there feels forced.

The question is always: **is this reader persuadable toward the PortCo?** If yes, and the editorial framing can do that work honestly, it's a CTR2 opportunity. Traffic without CTR2 is not a business outcome — but don't disqualify pages just because the PortCo doesn't make the exact product the keyword names.

---

### Step 5: Build the Content Roadmap

Sequence content in phases based on strategic priority:

**Phase 2A — Optimize and expand the core**
- Optimize the existing verifier page for organic ranking (title tag, meta, on-page structure, internal linking)
- Build 2–3 Ring 2 pages targeting the highest-volume adjacent keywords where the PortCo is a natural leader
- Start link building to the TPV domain (use the Link Building Strategy skill)

**Phase 2B — Build category authority**
- Complete Ring 2 coverage across all major category variants
- Begin Ring 3 informational content for topical depth
- Internal link architecture: all Ring 2 and Ring 3 pages link back to the core verifier page

**Phase 2C — Amplification**
- Ring 4 linkable assets to compound domain authority
- Refresh existing pages based on CTR2 performance data — which pages are ranking but not converting? Diagnose and fix.

---

### Step 6: Internal Link Architecture

Internal linking on a TPV has two jobs:
1. Pass authority from new pages to the core verifier page
2. Drive readers from supporting content to the pages where they'll see the PortCo and click through

**Architecture rules:**
- Every Ring 2 and Ring 3 page links back to the core verifier page (or to the most relevant Ring 2 page)
- The core verifier page links out to Ring 2 pages contextually — not in a list, but within the editorial narrative
- Never link out to the PortCo from every page — this reads promotional. The PortCo appears prominently in rankings and roundups, and the link to the PortCo is naturally embedded there
