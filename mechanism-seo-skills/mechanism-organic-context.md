## Definitions

Key terms used throughout this document and across all Mechanism organic work.

**PortCo** — Portfolio company. Any company that Mechanism owns or operates and that the Organic Team supports.

**Money Keyword** — A non-branded keyword with direct commercial intent that a target customer would search when looking to buy a product in our category. Examples: "best putting mat", "laundry detergent sheets". These are the keywords that drive revenue if we rank. They are the primary target for all organic strategy.

**Non-Branded Keyword** — Any keyword that does not include the PortCo's brand name. "Best putting mat" is non-branded. "PrimePutt putting mat" is branded. We focus heavily on non-branded because that's where new customers are searching — they don't know us yet.

**Branded Keyword** — A search query that includes the PortCo's brand name. Branded traffic is largely people who already know us. Important to monitor and protect but not the primary growth lever.

**Informational Keyword** — A keyword where the searcher is looking for information, not to buy. "How to improve your putting stroke" is informational. These can support SEO by building topical authority, but should only be targeted if they directly serve the business — not just to generate traffic.

**Supporting Keyword** — A keyword that is adjacent to a money keyword and helps build topical authority around it. Supports the money page's ability to rank without being a direct revenue driver itself.

**SERP** — Search Engine Results Page. What you see when you search Google. A SERP can include organic results, paid ads, product tiles, shopping units, AI overviews, video carousels, and more. The composition of a SERP determines whether ranking is worth pursuing.

**DR (Domain Rating)** — Ahrefs' measure of a website's overall backlink authority on a 0–100 scale. A useful signal but not the primary quality indicator. Relevance and real traffic matter more.

**TPV (Third-Party Validator)** — A Mechanism-managed domain that hosts editorial content targeting high-intent category SERPs. The primary use case is as a paid landing page — TPV pages intercept organic search traffic and serve as the destination for paid media campaigns, funneling high-intent visitors to a PortCo. The Laundry Guru (thelaundryguru.net) is the primary example.

**CTR (Click-Through Rate)** — The percentage of users who see a search result and click on it. Used in Google Search Console to measure how well a page is capturing impressions at a given position. A key indicator of whether a title tag and meta description are compelling enough to earn the click.

**CTR2 (Click-Through Rate 2)** — A Mechanism-specific metric used on TPV pages. Measures the percentage of users who land on a TPV page and then click through to the target PortCo. Tracks how effectively a TPV converts its traffic into PortCo visits. This is the primary performance metric for TPV pages.

**SERPdom** — The strategic goal of owning as many meaningful positions on a target SERP as possible — across the PortCo's own domain, TPV sites, and editorial placements on third-party sites. See the SERPdom Concept section for full detail.

**Halo Effect** — The indirect value that organic work generates across all other channels — paid media, direct, referral, and branded search — that doesn't show up in organic conversion attribution. One of the most undersold and misunderstood aspects of SEO investment.

**Link Gap** — The difference between the backlink profiles of competitors ranking for a money keyword and the PortCo's current backlink profile. Closing the gap is a core link building objective.

**Outreach** — The process of contacting relevant websites to secure editorial link placements or brand mentions. The highest-leverage and most time-consuming link building activity.

**AEO / GEO** — Answer Engine Optimization / Generative Engine Optimization. How a brand appears in AI-generated search answers (ChatGPT, Perplexity, Google AI Overviews). Our content and link tactics already feed this — we need better ways to measure and report on it.

**AOV** — Average Order Value. The average revenue per transaction at a PortCo.

**CVR** — Conversion Rate. The percentage of visitors who complete a desired action (purchase, sign-up, etc.).

**LTV** — Lifetime Value. The total revenue a customer generates over their relationship with a PortCo.

**RPV** — Revenue Per Visitor. AOV × CVR. Used to map organic traffic potential to revenue impact.

## 3x5 Card — Organic Team

*The non-obvious, high-consequence truths that must not be violated.*

**What We Know**

1. **Relevance beats DR.** A contextual link from a niche-relevant DR 50 site with real traffic outperforms a DR 80 general site with a random contributor post. Chasing high DR without relevance wastes outreach capacity and produces weak signals.

2. **The halo effect is real and routinely underattributed.** Organic work converts through paid media, direct, referral, and branded search — not just organic. PortCos that only measure organic conversions will systematically undervalue what we do and deprioritize the engagement at exactly the wrong time. This expectation must be set at kickoff, every time.

3. **SERP composition determines whether ranking is worth it.** A cluttered ecommerce SERP (product tiles, ads, shopping units, video) dramatically reduces click-through even at position one. Evaluating winnability without evaluating click capture leads to misallocated effort.

4. **Content and links are 90% of the answer.** ORM, CRO, social, AEO — these either flow from content and links being done well, or they sit outside our lane. Scope creep into adjacent functions deprioritizes the invisible work (outreach, monitoring, GSC analysis) that actually moves rankings.

5. **SEO is a compounding investment, not a monthly deliverable.** Month one results do not reflect the value of the work. PortCos who aren't set up to think long-term will churn before the compounding kicks in.

**What We Think We Know**

1. **TPV is a scalable SERPdom vehicle.** The Laundry Guru → Hey Sunday model worked. The hypothesis is that Mechanism-managed TPV domains can reliably intercept high-intent category SERPs where core PortCo domains can't compete short-term. Not yet validated as a repeatable system across multiple categories.

2. **Authoritative, relevant links are the primary signal for AI search.** The way PortCos surface in AI-generated answers tracks closely with editorial mentions from trusted category authorities. Building these links now is building AI search presence for the future. Hypothesis based on directional evidence, not yet formally measured.

## How We Think About Organic Growth

### The Competitor Benchmark Is Always the Starting Point

Every Mechanism company exists because there is a competitor out there we are trying to replicate and out-sophisticate. Organic strategy starts there.

When sizing a new opportunity:
1. Search the target non-branded money keywords in Google — see who is actually ranking
2. Pull the top competitor domains into Ahrefs — analyze traffic, link profiles, and page types
3. Find the competitor most similar to what the PortCo is trying to become
4. Map their non-branded keyword traffic against the PortCo's CVR, AOV, and LTV to size the revenue upside

We are not just sizing keyword volume. We are sizing **revenue upside by mapping competitor traffic against PortCo economics**. That is a meaningful distinction from how most SEO teams approach this.

### We Bring the Strategy — PortCos Don't Brief Us

We do not ask a PortCo what their money keyword or money page is. That is our job to know. We show up with the analysis, the strategy, and the direction. We ask them for:

1. **What's distinctive about the product** — because outreach only works when we believe in what we're selling. The best links come from putting a genuinely good product in front of the right people.
2. **How they measure organic performance** — GA4, Triple Whale, Shopify, whatever they anchor on. We align on this before work starts.
3. **Billing and expected deliverables** — we map organic traffic potential to business impact using CVR, AOV, and LTV. We bring the projection. We set the expectation that SEO is a long-term investment — Month 1 results do not reflect the value of the work.

### The Halo Effect Is Real and Undersold

When 20 different sites that are all authorities in a category are all talking about a PortCo as one of the best — that is the halo effect. It shapes how people behave across every channel.

A consumer who reads a Breaking80 article about PrimePutt being the best putting mat, then converts via a Meta ad two weeks later, shows up as a Meta conversion. That is not a failure of organic — that is the halo effect working exactly as intended. Our work bleeds into:
- Higher converting paid media
- More direct traffic
- More referral conversions
- Stronger branded search volume

If leadership only anchors on organic conversion attribution, they will systematically undervalue what we do. We set this expectation at kickoff — every time.

The CPC sanity check is a useful tool for paid-media-minded CEOs: "Here is what this traffic would cost you if you had to buy it."

## The SERPdom Concept

SERPdom is the goal of owning as many meaningful positions on a target SERP as possible — through the PortCo's own domain, through TPV sites, and through editorial placements on third-party sites that rank for the keyword.

The thesis is simple: the more authoritative sources talking about your brand in a category, the more likely a customer converts — across every channel, not just organic. If someone searches "best putting mat" and sees PrimePutt mentioned in three of the top ten results, that's SERPdom working.

**What SERPdom is not:**
It is not a precise revenue forecasting model. Older versions of this framework attempted to model CTR curves, position values, and revenue attribution per placement. That approach was built for a cleaner SERP era — roughly 3 paid links and 10 organic results. Modern SERPs include AI overviews, product tiles, shopping units, video carousels, and ads that compress organic click-through at every position. The model breaks broadly, not just in ecommerce. The attribution is also an unsolvable problem — a customer who reads a Breaking80 article and later converts via paid will never show up as an organic conversion.

**How to use it practically:**
SERPdom is a directional framework, not a formula. The right question is: *are we showing up in the authoritative places where our customer makes decisions?* Not: *what is the exact revenue value of position 3?*

When evaluating a SERP, ask:
- Who owns the top organic positions? Are any of them Mechanism vehicles (PortCo domain or TPV)?
- Which editorial sites are ranking that we could get featured on?
- What would it take to get the PortCo brand into 3+ of the top 10 results?

That last question is the SERPdom goal. The halo effect does the rest.

## How We Evaluate SERPs

### Winnability Signals

Before pursuing a money keyword, evaluate:

**Competitor signals:**
- DR of domains in top positions
- Domain age and establishment
- Page-level backlink count and quality (especially .edu and category authorities)
- Overall site traffic

**SERP composition signals:**
- How many clicks does this SERP actually capture? A clean 10 blue link SERP captures far more clicks than a cluttered ecommerce SERP with product tiles, ads, shopping units, and video
- Search intent fit — are people actually looking to buy what we sell, or just something adjacent?

**The key question is not just "can we rank" — it's "if we rank, does it actually drive conversions?"**

Example: "ADHD test" is high volume and theoretically rankable, but the intent skews toward people looking for a free quiz, not telehealth services. Not worth pursuing even if the DR gap is closeable.

### SERP Composition Matters as Much as Competition

A messy ecommerce SERP (product tiles, ads, video, shopping units) dramatically reduces click-through even at position one. Sometimes the right call is to de-prioritize the direct money keyword and instead target:
- A higher-intent modifier ("best laundry detergent sheets" vs. "laundry detergent")
- The editorial layer sitting on top of the SERP (Good Housekeeping at P1 → get PrimePutt featured there)

This is the SERPdom model: if we can't own the SERP directly, we dominate it by being in every authoritative source that does.

## The TPV Model

A Third-Party Validator (TPV) site is a Mechanism-managed domain that intercepts high-intent search traffic and funnels it toward a PortCo. The Laundry Guru → Hey Sunday is the clearest example.

**Important: TPV is a case study, not a formula.**

In an ideal world, money keywords always go to the core PortCo domain. TPV emerged as a strategy because The Laundry Guru started performing exceptionally well for non-branded laundry keywords. We build on what works.

When evaluating whether a TPV makes sense:
- Is the money keyword SERP too competitive or too messy for the core domain to win near-term?
- Is there an editorial layer we can build credibly on a separate domain?
- Can we run SERPdom strategy to get a TPV page into the top editorial positions?

TPV content and SEO strategy follow the same principles as core domain work. The goal is always to be in the places where the customer is already looking for a recommendation.

## Link Quality Philosophy

### Relevance Over DR — Every Time

A DR 50 niche site with ranking pages in your category outperforms a DR 80 general site with a random contributor post. Always.

What makes a good link:
- **Contextual placement** in real editorial content — not directories, not niche edits, not spray-and-pray outreach
- **Niche relevance** — the site is actually talking to our customer
- **Real traffic on the linking page** — if nobody reads it, the signal is weak
- **Editorial credibility** — a Golf Digest mention of PrimePutt is not comparable to a scholarship link on a college site, even if the DR is similar

### Links Are Brand Signals, Not Just Domain Authority Plumbing

The future of link value is brand signal in authoritative, relevant places. An authority site in your category calling out your product as one of the best shapes how AI search surfaces your brand. A scholarship link does not.

Scholarship links and directory links have some residual value for building out overall domain authority. But they are not what moves rankings in a competitive category and they are not what builds lasting brand trust.

### The Don't-Do List

- **No low-quality links** — if it feels even 1% spammy, skip it
- **No buying links**
- **No direct link exchanges**
- **No spray-and-pray outreach** — large campaigns are fine and expected, but every target must be relevant to the category. Volume is a tool, not a strategy.
- **No content for irrelevant keywords** — traffic that doesn't connect to the business wasn't worth creating. Example: an article about "cool things to do in a small bathroom" for a laundry brand might get impressions. It does nothing for the business.

## The Invisible Work

What drives results that PortCo CEOs never directly see:
- **Outreach and back-and-forth** — the volume of emails, follow-ups, and negotiations required to land a single quality placement
- **Ahrefs competitor monitoring** — ongoing analysis of what's moving in the category, what opportunities are emerging, what competitors are doing
- **Google Search Console monitoring** — tracking ranking progress, catching drops early, identifying pages gaining momentum

When scope creep pulls the team into ORM, social, or CRO work — this invisible work is what gets deprioritized first. That is the real cost of scope drift that never shows up in a billing conversation.

## Reporting — What We Communicate

In weekly or monthly updates, we communicate how we are pacing toward organic business growth. That includes:

- Traffic on money keywords and ranking movement
- Increases in brand search traffic
- Bottom-line conversions driven from organic
- Meaningful new link placements and their strategic significance

We explain the *why* behind the numbers, not just the numbers. And we always communicate within the halo effect frame — organic value isn't always visible in organic attribution.
